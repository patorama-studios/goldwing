Sleep Easy … 
The doctor that had been seeing an 80 year 
old woman for most of her life finally retired.  
At her next check up, the new doctor told 
her to bring a list of all the medicines that 
had been prescribed for her.  As the young 
doctor was looking through these, his eyes 
grew wide as he realized she had a 
prescription for birth control pills.  "Mrs 
Smith, do you realise these are birth 
control pills ?!?"  
"Yes, they help me sleep at night."  
"Mrs Smith, I assure you there is absolutely 
NOTHING in these that could possibly help 
you sleep!"  
She reached out and patted the young 
Doctor's knee. "Yes, dear, I know that.  But 
every morning, I grind one up and mix it in 
the glass of orange juice that my 16 year old 
granddaughter drinks...  And believe me; it 
helps me sleep at night."  
All Blacks 
Waimaru the Maori, a dedicated “All 
Blacks” supporter living in Sydney, 
visits a doctor a few weeks before an 
“All Blacks” test match complaining of 
pain in his groin. 
The doctor examines him and then 
says "Waimaru, I am afraid I have 
some bad news for you - you have 
testicular cancer" 
"What does that mean?”  Waimaru 
asks. 
"We will have to take your testicles."  
the doctor answers solemnly. 
"Pig’s arse!”  Waimaru exclaims  “I'm 
going to get a second opinion.” 
He visits another doctor who also 
diagnoses the same condition. 
"So what’s the treatment?”  Waimaru 
asks. 
The doctor replies  "We'll be taking 
your testicles." 
Waimaru says  “No way ... I'm going 
home to see my old family doctor!” 
Waimaru flew home to New Zealand 
the next day. 
The old family doctor, also a Maori, 
examines him, shakes his head and 
says  "Bad news Waimaru ... you have 
testicular cancer." 
Waimaru asks  "So what you gonna 
do?” 
“Well,”  his doctor answers,  "We gotta 
cut your balls off!" 
"Thank Christ for that!”  Waimaru 
answers in a very relieved voice  “them 
Aussie doctors wanted to take me test 
tickets off me!"

Hi Fellow Riders, 
Well the dust has settled over Leeton 
and another AGM is past history.  
Thank you Western Districts for all 
your hard work, you can now all sit 
back and have a well earned rest. 
To all those who put their hands up to 
fill the National Committee positions 
congratulations and a big thank you.  It 
is sometimes a thankless job but 
without you all we would not have the 
association we have. 
Our ride to Burrawang Pub was great 
and it was very heartening to see such a 
good roll up from our members.  We 
were joined by Ron and Kathy Leslie for 
lunch and, after lively table discussions 
on a variety of subjects, we all headed 
in for home in different directions 
around 2 o'clock.  Ray and I had a 
practice run towing the trailer in 
preparation for Leeton.  We were 
feeling pretty good about how good we 
were going on our first attempt until we 
stopped at Mittagong to wait for the 
rest of the gang only to discover we had 
lost the lid off the tool box!!  So we 
turned around and went back along the 
expressway to see, if by chance, we 
could find it.  I bet you can guess what 
the odds were. 
Out ride on Sunday 17 
th  April was to 
Bateman’s Bay and what a top day it 
turned out to be.  We (as in Ray and I) 
met Mark and Marg at Kiama and then 
rode to Steve Hoger’s to meet up.  We 
were joined by out newest member 
Peter Harvey (no, not that one) and we 
then continued on to the Soldiers Club 
and were met by Greg O'Plexus and "El 
Presidente"  Bob Carmody.  After a 
slight hold up while a busload of 
pensioners were unloading a very alert 
security guard stopped Steve Hoger and 
asked for some ID (we always thought 
he looked a bit suss and we were proved 
right).  Lunch, scenery and company 
was great but also all good things must 
come to an end and we all said out good 
byes and headed for home. 
 
Ride safely and as often as possible! 
 
Ray and Lesley

G’day 
 
The AGM at Mandurah was excellent.  
How’s that for an opening statement!  
Those of you who could not attend 
missed a good one (yes I will rub it in) 
that was highlighted by a great ride 
across the country, for those of us on the 
east coast ... or close to it. 
 
The ride is of course the reason we all do 
it.  It’s the love of motorcycling that 
makes us undertake a journey of 
approximately 10,000km ... and it was 
well worth it.  From the boat cruise 
through the man-made canals out to the 
estuary (with dolphins of course) to the 
light show on the foreshore, the main 
parade, the show n shine (again on the 
foreshore), the AGM dinner and 
presentation, the Sunday ride to 
Dwellingup, and the Sunday spit roast ... 
it was simply a very memorable 
experience. 
 
Memorable experiences also include the 
mozzies at Mandurah, the rain from 
Walpole to Bridgetown, the 35° heat on 
the Nullarbor, the $2.11 per litre price of 
petrol, the 55km/h NE wind, the poor 
kangaroos that lost their lives from being 
hit by GoldWings, the excellent meals all 
weekend, Alistair Third’s time travels 
(especially at Border Village), Steve 
Eslick’s rear brake caliper, Alan 
Springett’s laughter on Sunday night, 
Gary Cubbage’s “camper”, Greg Swan’s 
tyres and wheel bearings, Cecily’s 
birthday on Saturday night, Ray Bailey 
and Tim Riley on Brokenback Mountain 
(you had to be there!), Marty 
Woodward’s “drop”, Lewis Furner and 
Greg O’Loughlin trying to work out what 
my timber helmet holders were, the two 
Rays’ being the two Rays’, Ian Cochran’s 
pretty Vulcan with new Classic trailer, the 
Masons at the lodge who served us 
dinner, Ian and Suzy Hall’s spit roast 
(wow!), Peter Cherry winning Club 
Person of the Year, the Dove’s  
“workshop”, the Swan girls (who hated 
the airplane flight), the seat on Graham 
Merrick’s Kawasaki ZZR 1200, Robyn 
Strong’s “test ride” and the Honda test 
fleet that included three F6Bs. 
 
Take a breath. 
 
Peter Murphy won back the Oldest Long 
Distance Rider trophy that he won before 
when he was living in Perth and rode to 
Gunnedah in 2007, Steve Ward wasn’t 
there to stuff things up for me, the 
Langley’s and the Cherry’s drove their 
4WD’s along the Anne Beadell “highway” 
to get there, Richard Alexander’s Elite 
camper trailer broke its drawbar, Bob 
Carmody’s sidecar killed a roo that 
actuated the handbrake cable and 
overheated the axle bearing grease, the 
bacon and egg rolls for brekky were 
great, raffle prizes at the AGM dinner 
included espresso coffee machines, crock 
pots and toolboxes that are obviously 
easy to carry on GoldWings (not), Ian Hall 
blocked the traffic on the parade route, 
did I mention the mozzies?, the spit roast

at the sheer size of this wide brown land.  
There was a remarkable lack of roadkill

kill between Broken Hill and Yunta was 
“nothing”!

Busselton AGM in 2003.  I avoid 
Wilcannia by filling up at Emmdale 
(160km from Cobar) then Little Topah 
(70km from Broken Hill) and the road 
from Broken Hill to Peterborough is 
wonderfully “desert-like”. 
 
Ceduna was the next stop and tea at the 
pub on the waterfront was beaut.  Border 
Village has its own time zone, which 
confused the hell out of my riding buddy, 
Alistair Third.  The pints (568ml) of 
Carlton Draught are only $9.80 and the 
meals are great. 
Norseman caravan park has bugger-all 
grass for campers, so I had to avoid the 
gravel ... and the food at the BP servo 
was pretty ordinary.  The tree-top walk at 
Walpole was arguably better than ten 
years ago, and it rained so the bush 
smelled real good.  The road in and out is 
excellent for bikes too. 
 
Albany was wet, and it rained all the way 
to Bridgetown where I had lunch at a 
great little cafe that served a massive 
burger. 
 
I thought the entire AGM weekend was 
well organised and it’s a credit to the WA 
members and their helpers that it all 
went according to plan ... almost. 
 
The Honda test fleet proved quite 
popular, especially with rides on the 
three F6B’s that were there.  Woo hoo! 
 
Congratulations to the trophy winners 
and to the entire AGM team for a 
weekend I won’t easily forget. 
 
My trip back home was rushed due to a 
personal emergency, but it was not 
helped by a serious rear tyre failure on 
the freeway at Goulburn.  Story and 
photos will be in the next edition. 
 
A great many thanks goes to my riding 
companions, David Partridge, Graham 
Merrick and Alistair Third for sticking 
with me and doing the long days to get 
home quickly. 
 
Thanks guys!  Riding with you was 
fantastic.

The President’s Report 
Greeting fellow members 
 
This is my first crack at writing a 
president’s report so please bear with 
me.  I am still finding my bearings 
(contact Greg Swan ... he found his 
bearings at Mandurah – Ed) after arriving 
back home but by the time you read this 
we as a Natcom will well and truly have 
knuckled down and got stuck into 
whatever it is that needs getting stuck 
into. 
 
I would first like to commend the work of 
the West Coast Chapter in putting on 
what to me was another cracker of an 
AGM.  In particular I would like to 
acknowledge the efforts of Allan and 
Elke, Gary and Christine, Ian Cochrane, 
David Hall and special thanks to Ian and 
Suzy Hall.  The dedication and hard work 
of these members to organising the rides, 
meals and other activities was nothing 
short of tremendous.  To the other West 
Coast Chapter members and those 
Wingers associated with the West Coast 
chapter, well done and thank you. 
 
At the time of writing this report I 
understand that there are quite a few of 
our members still in WA either doing the 
north or south, although most of us are 
probably safe at home.  As yet I have not 
heard of any incidents where bikes have 
gone down but understand there were a 
few trailer dramas both on the way west 
and on the return home.  I have heard  
how lucky Dave Partridge and Robert 
Carmody were in staying upright after 
hitting skippys. 
 
We had a lovely trip over west with the 
weather being kind.  On arrival at 
Mandurah we found the park staff 
friendly and helpful and both of us 
enjoyed the opportunity to catch up with 
old friends and meet new friends as well.  
Our Quartermasters Sue and Peter 
Cherry despite having their own trailer 
difficulties let me know that we had a 
bumper order of AGA apparel from 
members which was great. 
 
I will leave it up to the respective 
chapters to give comprehensive details 
on the activities participated in but as far 
as Di and myself were concerned the 
food, activities and the fellowship was 
great.  Needless to say both Di and I had 
done the touristy bit around the southern 
bits of WA in the two weeks prior to the 
AGM event and we have had our appetite 
whetted in regards to coming back.  
There is way too much to see and do. 
 
As a result of the AGM we now have a 
National Committee with both new and 
experienced members.  On behalf of the 
Natcom I welcome our new National 
Secretary Melissa Eslick, and Allan Kerr 
and Richard Alexander as committee 
members.  I would like to again take this 
opportunity to thank Norm Francis for his 
two years as National President.  Under 
Norm's leadership we saw a few changes

This year the chapter decided to make 
the bold move to hold Sanity somewhere 
other than Cowra.  For years the chapter 
had used the Cowra Holiday Park to run 
the popular Sanity weekend, but a 
number of people, both inside and 
outside of the chapter, had said that it 
was getting “stale”.  When we thought 
about Sanity at Cowra we acknowledged 
that the trucks on the highway at Cowra 
were very noisy, the camp shelter was 
cold when it was raining or windy, the 
seats were not really comfortable, we 
had difficulty finding different rides, and 
if you were a pillion whose rider didn’t 
want to go anywhere, then you didn’t go 
anywhere either because the caravan 
park was 5 km out of town. 
 
We knew we had to make changes to 
ensure that people still wanted to attend 
Sanity, so the chapter members agreed at 
a meeting to change the venue – and 
Wellington Caves “got the nod”. 
 
Sanity was held on 18 – 20 October.  The 
weather was great all weekend and many 
of us took advantage of the café within 
easy walking distance to get a coffee, 
snacks or lunch on the Friday and 
Saturday.

sausages, so we quickly arranged for a 
gas barbecue, and the cooking began.

G’day 
 
Last month Tim Loader posed the 
question, “What is a real biker?” 
A real biker is one who rides his bike to 
work and back.  He rides his bike to the 
shop to get ‘stuff’.  He rides no matter 
what the weather is.  He doesn’t just 
ride for the enjoyment of it, but for the 
practicality of it … although riding to 
the bottle shop for a carton of bubbly is 
largely impractical.  He rides at every 
opportunity and has no qualms about 
riding long distances on a regular basis. 
 
I suspect few of us ride our GoldWings 
to work on a regular basis.  And I 
suspect that is basically because 
GoldWings are not commuter bikes.  We 
mostly drive our cars or other means of 
four-wheeled transport … for 
practicality. 
 
When I was growing up, a true biker 
owned a BMW boxer that was never 
clean and it showed the obvious signs 
of use, but it was maintained very well 
indeed.  Honda Four’s, Kwaka Nine’s, 
XS1100 Yammies and even some GS 
Suzuki’s were affordable bikes that one 
could commute, scratch and tour on.  
British bikes had their time and were 
not reliable enough.  Harleys did not 
even equate.  Moto Guzzi riders were 
real enthusiasts, as were early Ducati 
owners … but Dukes had reliability 
problems too.  Every Duke owner I 
knew carried duct tape, araldite and a 
soldering iron in the toolkit. 
 
Enthusiasts also rode Laverda’s, 
Benelli’s and Bimota’s, then there were 
the British Norton’s, Triumph’s and 
BSA’s that were ridden by die-hard men 
who were arguably real bikers in their 
day. 
 
All these bikes were used for touring 
before ‘true’ tourers were invented, 
although BMW was the exception with 
the R90S quickly followed by the 
R100RS and RT in the early seventies.   
 
Enter the Suzuki GS1000K, Kawasaki 
Z1100A and the Honda’s amazing GL1100 
GoldWing.  These bikes were purpose-
built for the long haul, but they were 
too heavy and cumbersome to regularly 
commute on.  However, the owners 
mostly did ride their tourers to work 
and to the shop and almost everywhere 
… in the days when it wasn’t so easy to 
afford a car as well.  When mum didn’t 
do paid work and mortgages didn’t 
require two incomes to pay them off. 
 
Nowadays there is a big difference 
between a true biker and true tourer.

Some lessons for the naïve and uninitiated: 
 Don’t use an old key unless you have had it resynchronised. 
 If you do use an old key, and your bike won’t start, check the HISS light. 
 Don’t run your battery to death if your bike won’t start, nothing is going 
to magically change. 
 When installing a new battery, check that you have the terminals in the 
positions. It’s not hard to do, the terminal leads give you a pretty good 
hint. 
 Remember that there is a big black fuse underneath the pretty coloured 
ones that is hard to see. 
 Don’t be scared to use the wealth of knowledge that your mates have – 
even the ones who ride a Harley; and finally, 
 Don’t call Wagga Wagga, Wagga.  The locals hate it.
